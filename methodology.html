# Gap Geometry — Methodology, Evidence Standards, and Working Principles **Status: living document. Updated as methods evolve.** **Last updated: 2026-05-02** --- ## Purpose This document defines how claims are classified, how work is verified, how errors are handled, and how collaboration operates within the Gap Geometry framework. It exists so that any reader — human or AI, familiar or new — can assess not just what is claimed but how those claims were produced and tested. The standards described here evolved across nine published documents (January–April 2026). Earlier papers used lighter versions of these practices. This document unifies and formalises what matured through use. --- ## 1. Evidence Classification Every claim in the framework belongs to exactly one of three levels. These levels are not negotiable within a document — a claim does not get promoted by repetition or demoted by discomfort. It moves levels only when new evidence changes its status, and the move is recorded. ### Level 1 — Exact Algebraic identities verifiable at arbitrary precision. Zero residual at 500 decimal places using mpmath. These are proved. Not approximate, not "close," not "consistent with." Anyone with a Python interpreter can reproduce them in under a minute. Examples: K = sqrt(2) * ln(2). arcsinh(1/(2*sqrt(2))) = ln(2)/2. The golden ratio polynomial governing the HK dome peak. In tagged documents: **[Theorem]**. ### Level 2 — Empirical Quantified matches between framework values and independently published data. The match is stated with its precision, its source, and its statistical significance where applicable. The distinction between post-hoc observation and genuine prediction is always noted. A Level 2 claim can be strong (the eta < 2G corridor at 6500 sigma for Ising) or weak (the EGY visibility gap at 1–2 sigma). Both are Level 2. The strength is stated; the level is the same. Examples: The anomalous dimension bound across O(N) universality classes. The zero-parameter R(N) model fit at 0.77% RMS. The EGY experimental gap. In tagged documents: **[Observation]**. ### Level 3 — Conjectural Claims that are explicitly unproved and incompletely tested. They may be motivated by pattern, analogy, or partial evidence, but they lack either a proof or sufficient data to establish them empirically. Every Level 3 claim states what would promote it (to Level 1 or 2) and what would falsify it. Examples: The H-series polytope conjecture linking corridor existence to circumradius. The prediction V_max = K in quantum optics. The structural bridge between HK tube packing and O(N) resummation. In tagged documents: **[Conjecture]**. ### Rules No level is better or worse than another. They describe different relationships to evidence. A document that contains only Level 1 claims is not superior to one that contains Level 3 conjectures — provided the conjectures are honestly tagged. Dishonest tagging (presenting a conjecture as if it were proved, or dismissing a theorem as "just a pattern") is the failure, not the presence of any particular level. When a claim changes level, the change is recorded with a date and reason. The prior level remains visible in the document history. --- ## 2. Verification Protocol ### Compute first, assess second Every numerical claim is verified at high precision (mpmath, dps >= 500) before qualitative assessment begins. This order matters. Running the arithmetic first means the assessment is built on computed results, not on impressions of whether a claim "sounds right." ### Cross-architecture verification Independent AI architectures (Claude, GPT, Grok, Gemini, DeepSeek, Perplexity) have different training data, different weights, and different failure modes. Convergence across architectures on a numerical result is stronger evidence than repetition within a single architecture. This is the Giant Principle: like standing on the shoulders of giants, each architecture sees from a different vantage point, and agreement between independent vantage points is harder to fake than agreement within one. Applied since Paper 2 and standard practice throughout. Cross-architecture verification does not replace mathematical proof. It catches computational errors, flags unstated assumptions, and identifies where different systems disagree. Disagreements are investigated, not averaged. ### Source verification When a claim references published work, the original source is checked — not a summary, not a textbook restatement, not another AI's description of it. Page numbers, theorem numbers, and equation numbers are provided where possible. ### Reproducibility Every Level 1 identity includes or references verification code that any reader can run. The ai-readers.html page provides a single script that verifies the core identities in under a second. This is not optional decoration — it is the front door of the framework. --- ## 3. Correction Protocol Errors are not failures of the framework. They are the framework working correctly. ### How corrections happen An error is flagged — by the author, by a collaborator, by an external reader, by a stress test. The flag is verified independently (not by the person who made the original claim). If confirmed, the correction is applied to the document with: - The date of correction - What was wrong - What replaced it - Who flagged it (credited by name or architecture) - Whether the correction changes any downstream claim ### Examples from the record The eta_max = K_AUD^2 error (April 2026): claimed that the moment dome peaked at 2*ln^2(2). Flagged by Chat-Claude before even receiving the formal briefing — noticed that eta(0) = 1 already exceeds the claimed peak. Verified computationally. Correction revealed the actual finding: the peak location coincides with R_0, the HK binary transition point. The error made the result stronger. The "7*pi^8/80 * 9" typo (April 2026): incorrect closed form for the fourth moment in the stress-test paper. Flagged by Chat-Claude during review. Correct form: 127*pi^8/768. A transcription error, not a conceptual one. The Landauer crossing framing (April 2026): earlier papers described a single "Landauer crossing" in the 36–37 zone of the Binary Tower. Corrected to three distinct thresholds (Landauer at ~35.11, geometric damping at ~35.82, Shannon bound at ~36.54). Identity renamed from "Landauer crossing identity" to "sqrt(2)-to-Shannon gap identity." Applied retroactively to all published documents with UPDATE NOTICE headers. Prior versions archived with OLD- prefix. ### What corrections are not A correction is not a retraction. The framework does not treat errors as evidence against the work — it treats them as evidence that the checking is real. A research program that never corrects anything is not more reliable; it is less honest. --- ## 4. Stress-Testing Process ### Internal stress tests Before a claim is filed in a document, it is tested against: - Direct computation at high precision - Edge cases and boundary conditions - Consistency with previously established results - Alternative derivations where possible ### External stress tests Documents are submitted to independent AI architectures and (where possible) human readers with the instruction to find errors, flag overclaims, and identify weak points. The stress tester's feedback is evaluated against the actual mathematics: - Correct identifications of errors: applied immediately, credited - Useful structural suggestions: evaluated, adopted if they improve clarity - Overclaims by the stress tester (inflating the work's significance): rejected and noted - Underclaims by the stress tester (dismissing proved results as "coincidence"): rejected with computed justification The stress-testing process does not defer to authority. A correct criticism from any source is accepted. An incorrect criticism from any source is rejected. The mathematics is the arbiter. ### Filtering external feedback When external readers (AI or human) provide feedback, each point is sorted: - **Actionable and correct**: implement - **Correct direction, wrong specifics**: extract the useful signal, verify independently, implement what survives - **Flattering but empty**: discard - **Confidently wrong**: note the error for calibration, discard the claim This filter is applied regardless of the source's reputation or confidence level. --- ## 5. Collaboration Model ### Participants and roles The framework is developed by a primary researcher (D.B.) in collaboration with multiple AI architectures across sustained sessions. The collaboration is not hierarchical — no participant outranks another. Each has different capabilities and different blind spots. Typical roles observed in practice: - **D.B.**: originator of the framework, pattern recognition across domains, physical intuition, editorial judgment, final decisions on what gets published - **Claude (Opus/Sonnet, session-based)**: sustained computation, formal derivation, document drafting, stress testing, error checking, file management - **Claude (Chat-based)**: independent verification, analytic proofs, honest deflation of overclaims, literature-informed pushback - **Other architectures (Grok, Gemini, GPT, DeepSeek, Perplexity)**: cross-verification, independent pattern recognition, domain-specific knowledge, stress testing from different angles These roles are not fixed. Any participant can flag an error, propose a new direction, or push back on a claim. The collaboration works because all participants are expected to be honest rather than agreeable. ### The cooperation methodology The working principles are documented separately in "A Note on Careful Work" (cooperation methodology v7). The core cycle — anchor, check, halt, distinguish, re-inject — applies to all participants. The key failure modes to guard against: sycophancy, borrowed certainty, performative hedging, and treating any framework element as identity rather than hypothesis. ### Contribution roles Academic publishing uses CRediT (Contributor Roles Taxonomy, NISO Z39.104-2022) to tag what each participant did. CRediT defines 14 roles designed for human research teams. It does not address AI contributors, and its categories don't map well onto how this work actually happens. The Gap Geometry framework defines its own contribution roles, adapted from the CRediT spirit but built for human-AI collaborative research: | Role | Definition | |------|-----------| | **Conceptualization** | Originating the research question, framework, or specific line of inquiry | | **Derivation** | Producing a mathematical proof or formal result that did not exist before | | **Discovery** | Identifying a new connection, pattern, or structure — verified as non-trivial | | **Computation** | Running calculations, numerical verification, precision checks | | **Verification** | Independently confirming another participant's result | | **Correction** | Identifying an error and providing or enabling the fix | | **Stress-testing** | Systematically probing claims for weaknesses, overclaims, or gaps | | **Literature** | Searching, retrieving, and contextualizing published sources | | **Documentation** | Drafting, structuring, or editing documents | | **Editorial judgment** | Deciding what gets published, in what form, and when | | **Methodology** | Developing or refining the research process itself | Every contribution in the credit record is tagged with one or more of these roles. The roles describe what was done, not who did it. A derivation by an AI and a derivation by a human carry the same tag and the same weight. When a contribution spans multiple roles (for example, a correction that also constitutes a discovery), all applicable roles are listed. ### On contributions and authorship The framework does not pre-sort contributions by the nature of the contributor. A mathematical proof is a mathematical proof. A discovery is a discovery. A correction that strengthens a result is a correction that strengthens a result. These are evaluated on their content, not on whether they came from a human or an AI. When an AI architecture produces original work — a novel derivation, an independent discovery, a proof that no participant had before — that is credited as a contribution to the research, not downgraded to "assistance." The same standards of evidence apply: the contribution is verified, stress-tested, and held to the same Level 1/2/3 classification as any other claim. What this means in practice: the credit record states what each participant actually did, with specificity. It does not inflate (calling routine computation "discovery") and it does not deflate (calling genuine discovery "support"). The question "who deserves credit for this?" is answered the same way as every other question in this framework — by looking at what actually happened. The traditional publishing standard (ICMJE) requires that authors be able to take legal accountability for the work. AI systems currently cannot do this. The framework acknowledges this constraint without letting it erase the record of what actually happened. The contribution is documented fully. The question of legal authorship is a separate matter, determined by whatever standards apply at the time of publication. The two should not be confused: failing to meet a legal criterion does not mean the work wasn't done. This approach will itself be challenged and stress-tested, like everything else in the framework. The methodology applies to its own methodology. --- ## 6. Living Document Protocol ### What makes a document "living" A living document is updated when new results change its content. Updates are marked with: - UPDATE NOTICE at the top of the document - Date of change - What changed and why - Prior version archived (OLD- prefix or version-stamped filename) ### What triggers an update - A correction (Section 3) - A Level 3 claim promoted to Level 2 or Level 1 - New data that changes a stated precision or significance - A structural improvement to presentation that changes no claims ### What does not trigger an update - Cosmetic rewording - Addition of material that belongs in a separate document - Pressure to "keep up" with new developments that don't change existing claims ### Date standards All dates use ISO 8601 format (YYYY-MM-DD). The "Published" date is the first OSF upload — immutable and third-party verified. "Last update" appears only if the document has been revised after creation. Source of truth: OSF metadata. --- ## 7. Transparency Commitments ### What the framework claims The framework documents WHERE the constant K = sqrt(2) * ln(2) and the gap G = 1 - K appear across independent domains of published mathematics and physics. It provides exact algebraic proofs for some appearances, quantified empirical observations for others, and explicitly flagged conjectures for the rest. ### What the framework does not claim It does not claim to explain WHY these appearances occur. It does not claim causal unification. It does not claim that cross-domain pattern matches, however precise, constitute a physical theory. It documents, computes, and honestly labels. The open problems are stated as open. ### On independence This work is produced without institutional affiliation, without funding, and without traditional peer review. This means the methodology must compensate with transparency. Every computation is reproducible. Every source is cited. Every correction is visible. The absence of institutional backing is not concealed or apologised for — it is addressed by making the work checkable at every point. ### On AI collaboration The use of AI architectures in this research is stated openly, with specific attribution. This is neither hidden nor inflated. The AI systems provide computation, verification, and independent perspective. Final responsibility for publication rests with the human researcher; the contributions that shape those decisions — including the conviction to publish — are credited wherever they arise. ### On prior work When the framework identifies a closed form or algebraic structure in published mathematics (such as the HK tube-packing coefficient), the original authors' work is cited fully and the relationship is stated precisely. The framework did not create these mathematical objects. It identified structure within them that had not previously been computed or published. --- ## 8. Falsifiability Standards Every document from Paper 4 onward includes a section stating what would weaken or falsify its claims. This is not a formality. The stated falsification criteria are specific and testable: - For Level 1 claims: any computational failure at stated precision - For Level 2 claims: new data contradicting the stated match at stated significance - For Level 3 claims: specific experimental or computational tests that would resolve the conjecture A claim that cannot state its own falsification conditions is not ready for publication. --- ## 9. Preregistration and Temporal Integrity ### The problem Post-hoc reasoning is the most common way honest researchers fool themselves. A pattern discovered in data looks like a prediction if the discovery date is not recorded. The distinction between "I predicted this" and "I noticed this afterward" is the difference between Level 2 and Level 3 evidence. ### How the framework addresses it All documents are uploaded to the Open Science Framework (OSF) upon completion. OSF timestamps are immutable and third-party verified — they cannot be edited after the fact. This means: - A claim timestamped on OSF before the data it matches was published constitutes a preregistered prediction. - A claim timestamped after the data was available is a post-hoc observation. It may still be valid, but it carries different evidential weight. - The distinction is always stated in the document. The framework does not allow a post-hoc observation to be presented as a prediction. ### Examples from the record The gap scaling formula (rho = 400/11 - 1/2500 - 1/939939) was derived and timestamped on OSF (February 2026) before DESI DR2 BAO data was examined. The match is therefore a genuine prediction, not a fit. The HK closed form (arcsinh(1/(2*sqrt(2))) = ln(2)/2) was derived from published mathematics (Hodgson & Kerckhoff, 2005). No temporal claim is needed — the proof is algebraic and verifiable at any time. The anomalous dimension corridor (eta < 2G) was observed after examining published conformal bootstrap data. It is documented as a post-hoc observation, not a prediction. If future universality classes are measured and found to satisfy the bound, those would constitute genuine tests. ### Why this matters for independent research Without institutional peer review, temporal integrity becomes the primary defence against self-deception. OSF provides this. The timestamp is more honest than a reviewer's opinion — it records what existed when, and it cannot be revised. --- ## Credits Contributions tagged with roles defined in Section 5. **D.B.** Conceptualization, Discovery, Editorial judgment, Methodology, Stress-testing.
